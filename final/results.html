How does the spread of cases in the US compare to the spread of cases in Wisconsin?

This plot shows a side-by-side comparison of the cumulative cases over time in the US and the cumulative cases over time in Wisconsin, along with a line in each figure representing the number of new cases in Wisconsin and US respectively. It is intuitively observable that the spread in Wisconsin and the US trend similarly, with the average increase in cases per week in Wisconsin being about 45% and the average increase in cases in the US being 51%.

How does the number of deaths in the US compare to the number of deaths in Wisconsin?

This plot shows a side-by-side comparison of the number of deaths over time in the US and the number of death over time in Wisconsin, along with a line in each figure representing the number of new deaths in Wisconsin and US respectively. With an average increase of about 53% in Wisconsin and the average death increase in the US being around 60%, it is safe to say that the virus is similarily deadly across the nation as it is in the state of Wisconsin.

How does the population of a county affect the amount of tested and positive inidividuals?

This plot is a response to the perceived lack of testing availability despite the widespread rate of infection. To analyze how well counties were doing as far as diagnosing and treating citizens, rate of deaths, infection, and testing were all compared to the population of a county. The basic trend is that as population increases, percentage of population tested decreases. The same trend can be seen in positive cases and deaths, as is to be expected because the less people are tested, the less people will be tested positive. Milwaukee, an outlier in population (nearly 1 million) so not included on the plot, has the highest infection rate, with 0.25% of the population testing positive and has had 1.34% of the population tested. Compared to Eau Claire, a much smaller population of 100,000 has had 0.2% more of their population tested and 0.23% less positive cases. This difference suggests that there is a benefit to widespread testing regardless of symptoms. Testing even those that do not appear ill will be able to give proper care and quarantine to all, overall reducing the number of positive cases. The scatter plot with multiple points is an effective display method for the data as it portrays a non-chronological relationship between population and certain statistics directly related to population, effectively illustrating trends.

How does the concentration of nursing homes affect the rate of Covid-19 related deaths?

It seems to be commonly reported in the media that nursing homes are breeding grounds for Covid-19, and lead to high rates of death due to the virus. This plot somewhat rejects that hypothesis. Looking at the counties with the highest rates of death per positive cases, such as Bayfield (25%), Buffalo (20%), and Marquette (25%), there are at maximum one nursing home in each of these counties. Locations with similar populations and lower death rates, such as Dane (5.4%) and Kenosha (2.03%). This debunks how many Americans perceive the illness, and is imperative knowledge for leaders to pass along to citizens that anyone can be affected severely by this disease, not just older folk in close proximities. The map is an effective tool for displaying this information as it is easy to see the county direct relationship between location of nursing homes and the percentage of deaths.

How does population density relate to the community spread of the virus?


The relationship shown in these plots illustrates how much population density may be affecting the spread of the virus. Wisconsin is fortunate in that many of its counties have little to no cases, so counties with less than 3 cases were excluded in attempt to keep the data relevant to locations where community spread is more likely to be happening. Its worth noting that most of the excluded counties did not have dense populations. The first plot gives a better idea of the relationship because Milkwaukee, the outlier, is excluded from the data. The second plot shows how much milwaukee changes the model. For every 100 more people per square mile, the first model predicts an additional .013% of the population would be infected. This suggests that the more densely populated the area, the more social distancing should be put into effect to counteract the trend. Out of the many non-obvious relationships between variables, we found this relationship to have the best explained variance score. It makes sense that it is hard to track down the cause of variance for covid-19 data because there are so many paramaters that come into play in spreading a virus. The traing group almost always overfit or underfit the data with the explained variance score for the first plot being 0.46 compared to 0.52 for the test. With milwaukee included, the data was always overfit or underfit, suggesting that more densely populated counties outside of wisconsin would have to be considered to better model the trend past 1000 people per square mile.

How has the rate of testing increased by county, and what has caused the change?

This animation displays the change in rate of testing in the past month in different Wisconsin counties. One thing to note is that even though the axis changes, the intervals measuring percentage of population tested stay relatively similar, so this animation is most effective for comparing counties to one another by relative population percentage increase or decrease. Dunn and Dane county have consistently high rates of population testing, ranging from .48% and .66% respectively on March 31, to 1.86% and 1.54% on April 24. The average of .61% increase in population tested over this period is beat by these two counties, showing quick action on the part of legislators and healthcare workers. It's also useful to note that the death rate in these counties is either average or significantly below the average of those with less testing availability, showing the benefit of widespread testing.

What is the Relationship between Testing and Positive Cases?


It may seem obvious that more testing is extremely correlated with more positive cases, but across counties, that is not necessarily true. As shown above, one county might test more of their population that another, yet not have nearly as high of an infection rate. The explained variance for this model was very low, with .078 for the training data and -.026 for the testing data. One explanation could be that certain counties are pushed to test more or less because of ideologies or culture, even though they have a lower infection rate than other counties around them.
